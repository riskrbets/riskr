How Blackjack Odds Work

By the Riskr team · 7 min read

Blackjack is the rare casino game where the house edge can drop below 1% — a fraction of what roulette or slots take. That doesn't make it a winning game, but it makes it the least losing one on the floor, and the reason is entirely structural. The dealer plays a robot's script, while you get real choices. Understanding both sides explains exactly where the edge comes from and how it moves.

The dealer plays a fixed script

Unlike you, the dealer makes no decisions. House rules force a rigid procedure: hit on any total of 16 or below, stand on 17 or above. No reading the table, no hunches, no adjusting to your hand. The dealer is a machine executing the same loop every time.

That predictability is the player's opening. Because you know exactly what the dealer will do, every one of your decisions can be optimized against a known opponent. In most casino games the house's behavior is irrelevant — the wheel doesn't care about your bet. In blackjack, the dealer's script is public, and you get to respond to it.

Your edge: you get to choose

The player has options the dealer doesn't, and each one is a lever for tilting a hand in your favor:

  • Hit or stand — take another card or hold, based on your total versus the dealer's up-card.
  • Double down — double your bet for exactly one more card, used when the odds strongly favor you.
  • Split — turn a pair into two separate hands, each with its own bet and chance to win.
  • A 3:2 payout on a natural blackjack — an ace plus a ten-value card pays you 1.5× your bet, a bonus the dealer never receives.

These choices, used correctly, are worth a great deal. They're the only reason blackjack's edge is small instead of brutal.

The dealer's edge: acting last

So why does the house still win on average? One quiet, decisive advantage: the dealer acts last. You must complete your hand first, and if you bust — go over 21 — you lose immediately, before the dealer draws a single card. If you both bust on the same round, you still lose. The dealer's bust never saves you, because yours already happened.

That "player busts first" rule is the entire house edge in blackjack. Strip it away and the player would actually be favored. It's why standing on stiff hands against a strong dealer up-card is so often correct: not because you expect to win by standing, but because drawing risks the immediate, fatal bust the dealer doesn't have to fear until your turn is over.

Key idea: the player's choices push the edge down; the dealer acting last pushes it back up. Where those two forces balance is the house edge — and with good play it lands under 1%.

Basic strategy: the math, memorized

Basic strategy is the single best move for every combination of your hand and the dealer's up-card, computed from the probabilities. It's not intuition — it's a fixed chart that someone solved with math, telling you precisely when to hit, stand, double, or split.

Following it perfectly is what drives the house edge down toward that famous sub-1% figure. Deviate — stand when you should hit, skip a double you should take — and the edge climbs. A typical player going on feel hands the house several percent. A basic-strategy player hands over a small fraction of one percent. Same game, same rules, very different cost, decided entirely by how the player's choices are made.

How the rules move the edge

Not all blackjack tables are equal. The exact rules shift the edge meaningfully, and the most expensive one is the blackjack payout. A table paying 6:5 instead of the standard 3:2 on a natural looks like a tiny change but is one of the worst bets on the floor.

Rule change Direction Rough effect on house edge
Blackjack pays 6:5 instead of 3:2 Worse for player +1.4%
Dealer hits soft 17 (instead of standing) Worse for player +0.2%
More decks (single deck → six or eight) Worse for player +0.5%
Double allowed after splitting Better for player −0.15%
Late surrender offered Better for player −0.1%

Read across that table and the lesson is clear: a 3:2, fewer-deck game where the dealer stands on soft 17 might leave an edge near 0.5%, while a 6:5, eight-deck, hits-soft-17 table can push it past 2% — four times worse for the identical-looking game. The cards are the same; the fine print is where the money quietly changes hands.
