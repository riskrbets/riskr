A Short History of Dice and Craps

By the Riskr team · 6 min read

Dice are among the oldest gambling tools humans ever made. Long before casinos, before printed cards, before written rules of any kind, people were throwing small objects and betting on how they'd land. Craps, the noisy dice game on today's casino floors, is the latest chapter in a story that stretches back thousands of years.

Knucklebones and astragali

The earliest dice weren't cubes at all. Ancient peoples used astragali — the small ankle bones of sheep and other animals, sometimes called knucklebones. These bones have an irregular shape with a few distinct resting positions, so they could be thrown and "read" much like dice, even if the outcomes weren't all equally likely. Archaeologists have found such bones, and later carved and six-sided dice, across many ancient cultures, which tells us the urge to gamble on a random throw is very old and very widespread.

Over time, dice became more standardized: cube-shaped, with pips arranged so opposite faces sum to seven, much like the dice we use today. That refinement toward a fair, symmetrical cube is what eventually made the clean 36-outcome math of two dice possible.

Why this matters for the game: craps only works because modern dice are (or are meant to be) perfectly balanced cubes. The whole payout structure assumes each face is equally likely — a fairness the ancient bones never quite had.

The game of Hazard

Craps descends most directly from an older dice game called Hazard, which was popular in Europe over several centuries. Hazard already contained the core ideas craps players would recognize: a first throw that could win or lose outright, a number set as a target to chase, and rolls that ended the round. The rules were more elaborate than today's game, but the skeleton — establish a number, then try to repeat it before a losing throw — is clearly the ancestor of the modern come-out and point.

The road to American craps

As Hazard traveled and was played in different places, it was simplified and reshaped. The version that took hold in America streamlined the older rules into something faster and easier to bank. Through the 1800s and into the 1900s the game spread widely, and it's generally credited with picking up much of its modern American form along the way. The result is the game we now call craps: fewer special cases than Hazard, a clear Pass-line decision, and a layout a newcomer can learn in a few rounds.

Street craps vs casino craps

For a long stretch, the game lived in two worlds. In street craps, players bet among themselves — one shooter, an informal circle, money changing hands directly, often with no table at all. In casino (or "bank") craps, the house banks every bet on a formal layout, with staff to run the game and a felt printed with all the available wagers. The casino version is what standardized the payouts and the now-familiar table; the street version kept the game's raucous, communal character alive.

How the modern layout came to be

The sprawling craps layout — Pass and Don't Pass lines, the Come area, the Field, the boxes for Place bets, the propositions in the center — developed to let a casino offer many different bets at once and resolve them quickly. Each section corresponds to a wager with its own payout and its own house edge. The layout looks intimidating precisely because it packs so many distinct bets into one table, but every region traces back to the same simple core inherited from Hazard: throw the dice, set a number, chase it before you seven out.

A note on honesty: the fine details of who changed what, and exactly when, are blurry and disputed across sources. What's clear and well supported is the broad arc — ancient bones to standardized dice to Hazard to American craps — and that's the story worth carrying.
