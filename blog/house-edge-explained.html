House Edge Explained

By the Riskr team · 6 min read

Every casino game has a quiet built-in advantage for the operator, and it has a name: the house edge. It's not cheating, and it isn't hidden — it's a small, mathematical skim taken from each bet, on average. Understand the edge and you understand why, given enough time, the house always comes out ahead. It's the most important number in gambling.

Edge and RTP: two sides of one coin

The house edge is the average percentage of each bet the operator expects to keep over the long run. Its mirror image is return to player, or RTP — the percentage that flows back to players. They always sum to 100%. A game with a 2% house edge has a 98% RTP: for every $100 wagered across thousands of plays, about $98 is paid back out and $2 stays with the house.

Crucially, RTP is measured over the total volume of all bets, not your session. A 98% RTP doesn't mean you'll end the night with 98% of your stake. You might double it or lose it all. The 98% is what emerges across millions of bets pooled together.

The edge as a per-bet skim

The cleanest way to picture the edge is as a small toll charged each time you bet. Wager $1 on a game with a 2% edge and, on average, you hand over 2 cents and get 98 cents of expected value back. Any single bet returns much more or much less than that — but average enough bets together and the 2-cent toll is exactly what's left on the table.

Key idea: the house edge is an average per bet, not a fee you can feel on any one play. You won't notice 2 cents vanishing. You'll notice it after ten thousand bets, when it's added up to a fortune.

How the games compare

Not all games skim equally. The edge depends entirely on the rules and payouts. Here's roughly where the common ones land:

Game Typical house edge RTP
Blackjack (good play) Under 1% Over 99%
Crash ~1% ~99%
Roulette (single-zero) ~2.70% ~97.3%
Roulette (double-zero) ~5.26% ~94.7%
Slots 2% to 10%+ ~90% to 98%

Blackjack with solid strategy is the leanest skim on the floor — which is exactly why casinos use shuffling, rule tweaks, and table limits to protect it. Slots sit at the other end: flashy, fast, and often carrying the steepest edge in the building. Two machines side by side can have wildly different RTPs, and it's rarely posted.

Why a tiny edge is so powerful

A 1% edge sounds harmless. The danger is in repetition. The edge applies to every bet, and bets stack up fast. Say you play a 5% edge game, betting $1 a round, 500 rounds an hour. You're not risking $1 — you're cycling money through the machine. Your expected loss is 5% of total wagered: 5% × $500 = $25 every hour, regardless of how lucky any one spin feels.

Now compare that to crash at a 1% edge: same 500 bets, expected loss around $5 an hour. Same money in motion, five times less bled — purely because the edge is smaller. The lesson isn't that a low edge is "winnable." It's that the edge sets the speed at which a bankroll drains, and more betting always means more total skim.
