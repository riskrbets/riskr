Risk Management Basics

By the Riskr team · 6 min read

"Risk management" sounds like a phrase for hedge funds and insurance actuaries, but at its core it's a simple, everyday skill: deciding how much you're willing to lose before you find out whether you will. The same handful of ideas apply whether you're sizing a bet in a game, deciding how much of a paycheck to set aside, or choosing whether to drive in a storm.

Start with the worst case, not the best

Most people daydream about the upside — the jackpot, the perfect outcome. Risk management flips that around and asks the blunt question first: what can I lose, and can I survive it? Before any decision involving uncertainty, name the downside in concrete terms. Not "it probably won't happen," but "if it does happen, here is exactly what I'm out." A loss you can absorb and shrug off is in a completely different category from one that wrecks you.

This leads to the oldest rule in the book: never risk more than you can afford to lose. It's a cliché because it's true. The goal of staying in any game — financial or otherwise — is to not get knocked out. You can't recover from a bet that takes everything.

Probability and impact are two different things

A common mistake is collapsing "how likely" and "how bad" into a single vague sense of "risky." They're separate axes, and the most dangerous situations live in different corners than people expect. A high-probability, low-impact event (a small loss that happens often) is just a cost of doing business. A low-probability, high-impact event (a rare loss that's catastrophic) is the one that actually ends people — and it's the one our intuition tends to ignore precisely because it "hardly ever happens."

  Low impact High impact
Likely Routine cost — budget for it Avoid or fix at the source
Unlikely Usually fine to accept The quiet killer — cap your exposure

The takeaway: judge a risk by both numbers, and pay special attention to anything in the bottom-right cell, where a rare event could do permanent damage.

Key idea: "It probably won't happen" is not a plan. If the impact is severe enough, even a small probability deserves a hard limit. Survival beats optimization.

Position sizing: the lever you actually control

You usually can't change the odds of an uncertain event, but you can almost always change how much you put on it. That's position sizing, and it's the single most powerful tool in risk management. Risking 1% of what you have on any one outcome means a string of bad luck stings but doesn't end you. Risking 50% means two bad calls in a row and you're done. Same odds, wildly different survival.

The principle generalizes: keep any single exposure small relative to the whole, so no one event — however unlucky — can take you out of the game entirely.

Diversify what you can

Putting everything into one outcome ties your fate to that one thing. Spreading exposure across many independent outcomes smooths the ride: the good and the bad partially cancel, and you're far less likely to be ruined by a single bad draw. Diversification doesn't turn a losing proposition into a winning one — if every option has a negative edge, spreading across them just gives you a more reliable loss. But when outcomes are genuinely independent, it's one of the few "free" reductions in risk available.

Set your limits before you're emotional

The cruelest part of risk is that the worst decisions get made in the worst moments — chasing a loss, doubling down on a streak, refusing to walk away. The fix is to decide the rules in advance, while you're calm: a maximum you'll risk, a point where you stop, a cooling-off rule. A limit set ahead of time is a promise from your rational self to your future, tilted-up-or-tilted-down self. Limits set in the heat of the moment tend to move exactly when you need them not to.
