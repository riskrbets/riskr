Bankroll Management

By the Riskr team · 7 min read

Most people think the secret to lasting at a game of chance is picking the right game or the right moment. It isn't. The single biggest factor in how long your money survives is something far more boring: how much you bet relative to what you have. Bankroll management is the discipline of controlling bet size so that ordinary bad luck can't end your night in three spins. It's risk-of-ruin control, and it matters more than any "system."

What a bankroll is

Your bankroll is the total pool of money you've set aside for play — not your rent, not your savings, just the amount you've mentally walled off as "this is for entertainment, and I accept it could go to zero." Drawing that line first is the foundation of everything else. A bankroll you can't comfortably lose isn't a bankroll; it's a problem waiting to happen.

Unit sizing: bet a small percent

The core technique is unit sizing: instead of betting random amounts, you define one "unit" as a small percentage of your bankroll and bet in those units. A common rule of thumb is 1% to 2% per bet. With a $500 bankroll, one unit is $5 to $10.

Small units exist for one reason — to let you absorb a losing streak without going broke. The math is unforgiving here. Bet big and a normal run of bad luck ends you; bet small and the same run is just a dip you ride out.

Bet size (of $500) Losses to wipe out How fragile
$10 (2%) 50 straight losses Very resilient
$50 (10%) 10 straight losses Shaky
$250 (50%) 2 straight losses One bad minute from zero

A ten-loss streak is unremarkable — it happens to everyone. Whether it's a footnote or a funeral depends entirely on your unit size.

Key idea: small bets don't change the odds, and they won't turn a losing game into a winner. What they buy you is survival — more rounds before variance can knock you out, and more time enjoying the game.

Bet size beats game choice

Players obsess over which game to play, but for staying power, how much you wager matters far more than what you wager it on. A player betting 1% of their bankroll on a high-edge slot will almost always outlast a player shoving 25% of theirs onto a lower-edge blackjack hand. The house edge grinds slowly; an oversized bet kills instantly. Get the bet size right and you earn the time for skill, mood, and luck to even matter.

Loss limits and session budgets

Unit sizing handles the per-bet risk; session limits handle the human one. Decide before you start:

  • A session budget — the most you'll put in play this sitting (say, 20 units). When it's gone, you're done, full stop.
  • A loss limit — a hard floor where you walk away, set before the emotions of a bad run can talk you out of it.
  • A win cap (optional) — a point where you bank a good night instead of feeding it all back.

The enemy these rules defend against is chasing: raising your bets to "win it back" after a loss. Chasing is how a small, planned loss becomes a large, unplanned one. A pre-committed limit takes the decision out of your worst moment and hands it to your calmest one.

Keep "play money" separate in your head

Money you can afford to lose should feel different from money you can't. Mentally — and ideally literally — keep your bankroll in its own bucket, apart from bills and savings. When play money and real obligations blur together, every bet starts carrying weight it was never meant to, and that's when people make the worst decisions. A clean mental wall keeps the game a game.
